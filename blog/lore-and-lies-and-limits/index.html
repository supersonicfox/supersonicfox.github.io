Lore and Lies and Limits

Mar 7th, 2015

Tennis has much lore that is simply incorrect. It may have applied when players in the royal court played in England, but it has nothing to do with modern tennis. Unfortunately it is still taught and some tennis players will spend their lives playing ‘not tennis’.

Tennis also needs lots of lies. They aren’t really sincere lies, they are short-term ‘fibs’. They make you think about tennis the right way, but eventually the lie has to be revealed because you can’t get better by believing the lie anymore. When I played in Indian Wells, I encountered one of the lies head-on, and needed to let it go or I couldn’t get any better.

The particular lie was that tennis serves are hit ‘up’. You are short. The net is tall. You must hit up to get the ball over the net. This lie is very much true for a long time in tennis. At 60MPH it is true. Without a jump serve that gets the racket three meters off the ground, it is true. But as your serve speed and height increases, it is a lie. If you could shoot bullets off your racket, you clearly can not aim ‘up’ or the bullet would never land in the vicinity. No, if you are shooting bullets, you must aim ‘down’. Your service motion is ‘up’ but the angle of your body and your racket compared to the court must be downward. Or that ball is never coming down in time to get into that service box.

What angle downward? Well, right above the net kind of downward. And the actual ratio is seven-to-one (one meter fall/rise for every seven meters forward), and the angle is a bit downward at 82 degrees. So basically your body needs to angle inward a few degrees, and then you hit ‘up’ at the ball, but it comes off your racket neither up or even flat, but downward. If the ball bounces off the racket differently from 90 degrees, you may have to compensate even more.

The angles are not important, what is important is you can control the range. You must be able to hit the tape with your serve and your racket. Even a few inches below the tape. Putting all the energy you have into the serve: you are not de-powering to land this serve. You can’t win Indian Wells by de-powering to fix a problem, you must control your power. Then depowering is a timing tool, not a crutch.
