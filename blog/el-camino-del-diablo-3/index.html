El Camino Del Diablo (3)

Oct 24th, 2014

Continuing the road to the devil…

Serve ’D’

I have no second serves…. except for one. It isn’t always a second serve, but it’s purpose is to relieve me. Relief is very useful when you are stressed. And although I am a pretty laid-back tennis player outwardly, I can get very annoyed at myself and my inadequacies.

How inadequate? Well for many months I couldn’t manage to toss the ball properly. Toss…. toss… toss… In club tennis you can pretty much just keep tossing because people don’t play with a time limit. But eventually I would just get annoyed and not want to stop play. So I would hit the one serve I practiced more than any other until about month-nine. A drop forehand. Simply hit.

A Forehand?

Yes, a drop forehand is perfectly legal. It is also very relieving because it is something so practiced that you just “do it”.

Actually now I can do some pretty powerful ’D’ serves, but the goal isn’t to win, it is just to get the ball in play. It does that very nicely.

And some of my partners have even been aced by it!

Serve ‘E’

Serve ‘E’ had the most strange development of all my serves, and ultimately it developed all the way to obsolescence. I do not use it at all any more… I don’t even practice it any more… but my entire touch game is based on understanding this “serve”.

Serve ‘E’ is a slice done shoulder-height. You toss the ball out into quadrant I at approximately 45-degrees and you slice it. And by slice, I mean every essence of your mind is thinking and most of your body is doing ‘slice’, ‘perpendicular’, ‘rotations’, ‘insane’, etc.

Step-1

Step-1 is just getting the idea that the ball is out in quadrant-1 and you are slicing it. If you instinctively get really good at Step-1, the ball barely moves forward. Ultimately I think I did things in slightly the wrong order and serve ‘G’ would have been better to focus on first, but this is a minor nuance because both serves are really about understanding ‘Step-2’ so you can go back to ‘Step-1’ and get it right.

Step-2

The problem with Step-1 is you don’t have faith. If every part of your body is dedicated to slicing, the ball does not go forward… at all. It just spins in place.

So we need the ball to go forward but your arm isn’t going to be helping. But above I didn’t say all, I said most. The part of your body that will make the go forward is the one part that can’t help with slice. Your legs and hips. These we need to rotate. Rotate forcefully enough to propel a ball forward over the net.

That isn’t actually that hard: attach a racket to your hips and you can serious hit a ball. But you need to seriously rotate.

In my case John first made me toss a ball a bit too far away and rotate with my right leg stretched out… spinning like a top around the left foot. This may have helped me start thinking right, but it lasted only a week. The next week I was to keep my right foot in and think more like ‘Flying Superman’ with my bodies and limbs (except the left leg keeping me in the air)… but the rotation was still there.

The reasons for the spin are (a) to get contact with the ball that will propel it forward, (b) to make insane slice easier (effectively the ball is moving toward you by you spinning and that is easier to slice than not-moving), and © to get your body out of the way of the insane power slicing racket.

Step-3

Now combine Step-2 back with Step-1 and make sure to still do Step-2 to get your lower body helping out, but focus on Step-1 and slice the ball where that is all your slice is about… slice. rotations… insane rotations…

The toss is still about 45 degrees into Quadrant-I, but it will be pretty close to your body… about a forearms length out.
