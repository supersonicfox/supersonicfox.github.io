Stretching and Warming Up

May 7th, 2015

Tennis is not a terribly demanding sport initially, but over time you will be asking your body to do more and more unusual things. It is important you can decelerate properly: that is probably the number one source of injury. You “rip a serve” and then stop your serving arm abrubtly, and the jolt to your muscles will cause all kinds of issues â€“ now or later.

Similarly, you need to do some kind of warming up. You don’t actually warm your muscles / body, but you get them / it used to what you are going to do. This can be a combination of ‘stretching’ (motions that are not innately tennis motions) and ‘warming up’ (motions that are but are done ‘more lightly’).

Stretching

My main stretches are related to serving: I need to make sure my torso is comfortable going the range I ask it to go on a serve. I rotate my core as far as I can push it comfortably… then a little more… but avoid anything getting truly mad at me. Same over my back. Then I combine the two into a full tennis coil.

I also ‘dance’ on the court which gets my lower body used to the directional control and motions that will happen later. By ‘dance’ I mean jumping, hopping, side-stepping, cross-overs, shuffles, etc. Any normal or slightly unusual tennis move.

Warming Up

For warming up, I do all my main motions without a ball, and tosses without hitting them. It is a total of about five major motions on each side:

  • Top spin
  • Lower Slice
  • Heavy Pronation Snaps (essencially downward)
  • Pronation ‘mid-shot’
  • Higher Slice (snapping and not)

And also the pronation for service.

I do all of these without a ball and work up to medium-high velocities or higher depending on the motion needing resistance to be safe at full speed.

Consistency and taking your time

Whatever rhythm you do on tennis days, be consistent and as thorough as necessary to be safe. One day I tried to stretch “quickly” so the game could begin quickly… don’t do that. Be consistent or your body will be unhappy.
