Serving Stage Two

Jan 30th, 2015

I finished discussing the way of the devil with the hell (H) serve. There are actually a number of serves after the hell serve, but instead of boring you with lots of serve motion variations, I will bore you with lots of ball positions. As I get ready for Indian Wells, I am losing serves. Most professional tennis players have incredibly dangerous forehands. You should not hit to them. Nasty Slice Serve? Yes, that sets up for a perfect death stroke to your left or right as the right-hander’s forehand picks and chooses. What rattles a 4.0 and below does not rattle a 6.0 and above. They will be there and if they are there with a clean forehand, you have just lost the point.

So, some of my serves like ‘E’ are gone. And doing an ‘H’ on the ad-side is gone. And my ‘A’ was always a practice serve. And a wide ‘C’ serve on the deuce side is gone. Overall, I lost about four out of eight primary serves. If the opponent is a lefty, I switch some of the losses and end up back at four. Four serves isn’t a lot of serves. Even if my hell serve, my wide ‘C’ on ad, and my J1 serve are on the mark every time, my opponent would just get used to them. This is not a good thing.

But then John steps in and levels me up. Serves have a motion, and that motion is usually most easily understood when you toss the ball in a particular place. The hell serve is in front and directly above my head. Slice is way to the right. Loopy topspin falls behind me. But learning a serve motion and using a serve motion are not the same things. As soon as you have learned the serve motion, you can use it in many different ball toss situations. John (and now I) divide the world into 5+ locations. Three is above your head. Two is headed toward your serving arm. And one is almost parallel to your head and wider than your shoulder on your serving arm’s side. Four and five are basically the same going away from your serving arm. And six pushes that even further.

So if I just fell to four good service motions, I now have 3 or 4 ball tosses I can use with each. And each produces a different behaved ball. 3H vs. 2H vs. 4H are all different. 2J1, 3J1, 3J2, and 4J2 are all different. And now that I have enough variations, I can even keep you honest and throw in a forehand attack. It might be rare, but you slide or expect too much, and a 4H or 3E to your forehand will do something very interesting.

I still don’t normally use a change-up: where I put different amounts of energy into the serve for timing confusion. But with sixteen serves, they naturally change up on their own. And I can always work on that next.
